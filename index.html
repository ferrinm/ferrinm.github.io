purified protein approach

in vivo approach

  • trial experiments on different microscopes indicate our airyscan should work well for 3D quantitative characterization of condensates over time
    • typically 5-10 s per ~ 30μm X 30μm X 10 μm volume, single channel
    • minimal photobleaching even after ~10 minutes continuous imaging

  • example 3D max projection

    • I was initially worried about splitting events being caused by actin polymerization, but Tyler pointed out this is actually pretty exciting since it makes fusion events much more common and therefore more measurable; no other condensate system common in the literature has this feature
  • LatA-treated cells do not passively split and fuse condensates, but they will likely be useful for FRAP experiments since the cytoplasm is much less active

Vinu's modeling project

project overview

  1. modify my original simple model of CME with a condensate to make a constant coated membrane curvature instead of constant area
  2. perform similar analyses as I have already done to determine what the contribution from the condensate is:
    • when is it assistive to internalization?
    • when does it stall internalization?
  3. determine sensitivity of contribution to relevant parameters
    • volume of condensate
    • interfacial tensions of condensate
    • membrane tension
    • polymerization energy of clathrin coat
    • turgor pressure
  4. if time allows, characterize material properties of in vivo ede1 cytoplasmic condensates in order to constrain model parameters
    • contact angle with membrane can inform about relative surface tensions
    • FRAP speed can inform about viscosity
    • timescale of merging/splitting events can inform about relative surface tension/viscosity

updates

  • Vinu is mostly finished with constructing modified model, currently transcribing it into code in order to plot results
    • sanity check plots of individual components look correct so far
